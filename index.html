Introduction

  

  
In Formula 1, achieving peak performance on a circuit requires an understanding of the "optimal racing line," which is the trajectory that minimizes lap times, this line is influenced by aerodynamic forces, tire grip, weight distribution and track conditions.
Understanding the racing line involves applying principles from physics including, equations of friction, motion and centripetal forces as well as analysing real-world data.
  




 
Centripetal Forces and Dynamic Cornering

  

  
Formula 1 cars use the centripetal force to manoeuvre around turns at incredibly high speeds during a race.
The inward force needed to maintain a Formula 1 car's motion along a curved trajectory is known as centripetal force.
Without it, the car would continue to proceed in a straight path due to inertia, which would cause a collision. 
The magnitude of this force is given by F = mv^2/r. 
Where m, is the mass of the car, v is the velocity and r are the radius of the turn. 
From the diagram, we can see the direction of the centripetal force is always perpendicular (at a right angle) to the direction to the car. 
Since it changes the velocity's direction instead of its magnitude, it’s always perpendicular therefore does not change the speed. 
Centripetal force derives from the frictional contact between the tyres and the track surface. 
This force is dependent on both the normal force (N) and the coefficient of friction (μ) which can be seen from the equation: F = μN.
